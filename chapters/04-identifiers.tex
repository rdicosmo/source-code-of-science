\chapter{Identifiers for digital objects}
\label{ch:identifiers}

% Conceptual core. Thesis: the word "DOI" silently bundles FIVE separable concerns, and most
% arguments about identifiers are really arguments about one of the other four, mislabelled.
% Untangle them first; then intrinsic vs extrinsic falls out cleanly, and the SWHID with it.

\takeaway{Identification is one thing; location, metadata, organization, and curation are four
others. Keep them apart.}

\section{Five concerns hiding behind one word}
% TODO draft: motivate with the everyday confusion, then separate the concerns. When people
% say "DOI" they usually mean some mixture of all five below.
When we say a digital object \enquote{has a DOI}, we are quietly running five different concerns
together. Pulling them apart is the single most clarifying move in this whole subject:

\begin{description}[leftmargin=!,labelwidth=2.5cm,font=\bfseries]
  \item[Identification] assigning a unique name to an object --- the identifier proper.
  \item[Location] a mechanism to access or resolve the object (DOI $\to$ Handle $\to$ landing
        page; \textsc{swhid} $\to$ archive). The ISBN names a book; the library tells you where
        it sits on the shelf --- two different jobs.
  \item[Metadata] what is known \emph{about} the object, typically held in a registry.
  \item[Organization] who maintains the coherence of the system --- governance, the registry
        operator, the institutions that keep it alive.
  \item[Curation] the \emph{quality} of the metadata: how it is ensured and maintained, who
        decides which objects deserve metadata (or even identification), and how that is checked.
\end{description}

\section{``Crossref DOIs are better than DataCite DOIs''}

% asserting specifics in prose.
The claim is shocking to anyone who knows that, \emph{technically}, a DOI is a DOI: Crossref and
DataCite both mint identifiers on the same Handle system, and the identifier technology is the
same on both sides. It is obvious to anyone who knows that the two \emph{organizations}, and the
\emph{curation} they practise, are not the same at all. The sentence is not really about
identification --- it is about organization and curation, wearing the identifier's clothes.
That is the confusion the five concerns dissolve.

\section{Intrinsic versus extrinsic identifiers}
% TODO draft from: swh-pids.org::#diovsido,#swhexamples. Refs: swhipres2018, cise-2020-doi.
% DIO (extrinsic) vs IDO (intrinsic); three criteria: gratis, integrity, no middle-man.
An \emph{extrinsic} identifier delegates identification to an organization: it means nothing
without the register that backs it. An \emph{intrinsic} identifier is computed from the content
itself, so it needs no organization to \emph{mean} something --- only, separately, to
\emph{locate and preserve} what it names. That one separation is the whole story: it buys
integrity, it costs nothing, and it removes the middle-man~\autocite{swhipres2018,cise-2020-doi}.

\takeaway{An intrinsic identifier is computed from the content; an extrinsic one is assigned by
a registry.}

\section{The SWHID}

% Types + granularity (snp/rev/rel/dir/cnt/lines); qualified SWHIDs; standard status.

The \textsc{swhid} is the intrinsic identifier for source code --- an international standard,
ISO/IEC~18670:2025, also referenced by SPDX, registered as the IANA \texttt{swh:} scheme, and
recorded as Wikidata property P6138. Its syntax and granularity are summarised in
Appendix~\ref{app:swhid-syntax}. This note is itself named and verified this way: the Colophon
shows how a document can refer to its own archived source, and why a hash forbids it from
\emph{containing} its own identifier.

\begin{handson}[Hands-on: watch the name follow the bytes]
\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
  \item Compute a content \textsc{swhid} for a file: \texttt{swh identify file.c}.
  \item Change a single byte and recompute --- the identifier changes completely.
  \item Restore the byte; the original identifier returns, to the character. No registry was
        ever consulted, and no authority was asked --- the name simply follows the bytes.
\end{enumerate}
\end{handson}
