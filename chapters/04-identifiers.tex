\chapter{Identifiers for digital objects}
\label{ch:identifiers}

\begin{objectives}\small
\begin{itemize}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
  \item separate the five concerns bundled into the word ``identifier'';
  \item contrast intrinsic and extrinsic identifiers, and say why it matters;
  \item read and build a \textsc{swhid}, in both its core and qualified forms.
\end{itemize}
\end{objectives}

The previous chapter left us with a quietly remarkable fact: in the Software Heritage data model,
every artifact already \emph{has} a name, computed from its own content. Before we put that name
to work, we should be clear about what a name is \emph{for} --- because almost everything people
believe about digital identifiers is tangled in a confusion that a few minutes of care can
dissolve.

\takeaway{Identification is one thing; location, metadata, organization, and curation are four
others. Keep them apart.}

\section{Five concerns hiding behind one word}

When we say that a digital object \enquote{has a DOI}, we are quietly running five different
concerns together. Pulling them apart is the single most clarifying move in this whole subject:

\begin{description}[leftmargin=!,labelwidth=2.5cm,font=\bfseries\sffamily]
  \item[Identification] giving the object a unique name --- the identifier proper.
  \item[Location] a way to \emph{access} or resolve the object. The ISBN names a book; the library
        catalogue tells you which shelf it sits on. Two different jobs.
  \item[Metadata] what is \emph{known about} the object --- authors, title, date --- usually held
        in a registry.
  \item[Organization] who keeps the system coherent: the governance, the registry operator, the
        institutions that keep the lights on.
  \item[Curation] the \emph{quality} of the metadata --- how it is checked and maintained, and who
        decides which objects deserve metadata, or even a name, at all.
\end{description}

Only the first is \emph{identification} (Figure~\ref{fig:five-concerns}). The other four are real
and important --- but they are not the same thing, and most arguments about identifiers turn out to
be arguments about one of the latter four, wearing identification's clothes. One example makes this
unforgettable.

\section{When is one DOI ``better'' than another?}

Here is a question that sounds simple and turns out to be a perfect test of the distinction: are
the DOIs minted by one registration agency \enquote{better} than another's --- Crossref's, say,
than DataCite's? The honest answer is \emph{yes and no} at the same time, and which half you land
on depends entirely on which of our five concerns you have in mind.

At the level of \emph{identification} there is no difference to find. A DOI is a DOI. Crossref and
DataCite are both \emph{registration agencies} accredited by the same International DOI Foundation;
both mint identifiers resolved by the same underlying Handle System~\autocite{Arms01,RFC3650},
through the same \texttt{doi.org}. They are so much one system that when \texttt{doi.org} suffered
an outage in 2015, it took down \emph{every} agency's DOIs at once. Hand someone a bare DOI and, as
a rule, they cannot even tell you which agency registered it. As \emph{identifiers}, then, they are
the same kind of object, minted and resolved in exactly the same way~\autocite{doifoundation_ra};
\enquote{better} has nothing to bite on.

And yet the question is not idle, because there \emph{is} a real difference --- it just lives in
\emph{organization}, \emph{metadata}, and \emph{curation}, not in the identifier. Crossref grew up around the
scholarly literature, DataCite around research data and software; the two run different metadata
schemas, serve different communities, and accumulate metadata of differing richness. But that
richness, as the two organizations themselves put it, \enquote{hinges on what depositing members
actively provide, rather than being automatically generated by the DOI assignment process
itself}~\autocite{crossrefdatacite_metadata}. The quality lives in the organization and its
curation, not in the identifier. So the answer is yes \emph{and} no --- and it sounds paradoxical
only because all five concerns were crammed into one word. Pull them apart and the paradox
evaporates: as \emph{identification}, no DOI is better than another; as \emph{organization, metadata,
and curation}, they can differ a great deal.

\section{Intrinsic versus extrinsic identifiers}

There is a deeper lesson hiding in that example, and Norman Paskin --- long the voice of the DOI
itself --- put his finger on it: \enquote{Digital Object Identifier} is best read as \emph{digital
identifier of an object}, not \emph{identifier of a digital object}~\autocite{paskin2010digital}.
That distinction is the whole game.

An \emph{extrinsic} identifier --- a DOI, a Handle, an ARK --- is a digital identifier \emph{of}
something, assigned by an organization and recorded in a register. It says nothing about the bytes
it names; it is a pointer whose meaning is maintained, on goodwill, by several parties at once. The
resolution can be repointed; the content at the far end can change; and there is \emph{no intrinsic
way to notice}~\autocite{swhipres2018} (Figure~\ref{fig:doi-resolve}). Anyone who has clicked a DOI
and met \enquote{not found} has felt the consequence. Such an identifier delegates to an organization
not only \emph{location} but \emph{identification} itself: without the register, it means nothing.

There is a subtler cost still, and it lands on the very word that sells these identifiers:
\emph{persistent}. Persistence is not a property the technology confers. The Handle System is the
machinery beneath the DOI, and its own specification concedes as much: resolving a handle
\enquote{does not guarantee persistence, which is a function of administrative care}, and
\enquote{the persistence of handles depends more on administrative policies than the technology
itself}~\autocite{RFC3650}. The \enquote{P} in \emph{persistent identifier}, then, is a promise
kept by an \emph{organization}, not a guarantee written into the name: a DOI resolves for exactly as
long as someone keeps minding the register and paying for the service, and the day that care lapses
it is as mortal as any URL. Persistence is administered, never computed.

An \emph{intrinsic} identifier --- a Git commit hash, a \textsc{swhid} --- is an identifier
\emph{of} a digital object, computed from the object's own bytes. It needs no organization to
\emph{mean} something; it needs one only, and separately, to help you \emph{locate} what it names.
As we saw in Chapter~\ref{ch:foundations}, this is exactly what the Merkle construction buys:
identifiers that are gratis, that guarantee \emph{integrity}, and that need \emph{no
middle-man}~\autocite{cise-2020-doi}. Map this back onto our five concerns and the elegance is
plain: an extrinsic identifier entangles identification with location, metadata, organization, and
curation all at once; an intrinsic identifier \emph{separates} identification from all four. That
separation is no technicality. It is the difference between a name you must trust someone to keep,
and a name anyone can check.

\takeaway{An intrinsic identifier is computed from the content; an extrinsic one is assigned by a
registry.}

\section{Who must be trusted, and for what}

That last sentence deserves more than rhetoric, because \enquote{trust} does a great deal of quiet
work in every argument about identifiers and is almost never unpacked. Let us unpack it. Naming a
digital object and later getting it back involves four separable acts of faith:

\begin{description}[leftmargin=!,labelwidth=2.5cm,font=\bfseries\sffamily]
  \item[Minting] someone must issue the name --- and never issue the same one twice.
  \item[Resolution] someone must keep running the machinery that turns that name into a location.
  \item[Integrity] someone must ensure the bytes you are handed are the bytes that were named.
  \item[Availability] someone must still be holding those bytes when you come looking.
\end{description}

\noindent Every identifier system is, in the end, a claim about which of these four you may stop
worrying about. The useful question to ask of one is therefore not \enquote{is it persistent?} but
\emph{whom am I trusting, and for which of the four?}

The identifier literature asks this in terms of the \emph{mechanisms} a system offers. Three are
common to any system worth the name: \emph{generation} of a label, \emph{assignment} of that label to
an object, and \emph{retrieval} of the object from the label. Three more are optional:
\emph{verification} --- checking that a label and an object actually correspond --- \emph{reverse
lookup}, recovering the label from an object in hand, and \emph{description}, fetching the object's
metadata~\autocite{swhipres2018}. Survey the familiar systems against that list, and one row is
conspicuously empty (Table~\ref{tab:mechanisms}).

Read that table slowly, because it is this chapter in miniature. \emph{No} classical
persistent-identifier system provides verification. Not one of them lets you ask, from the identifier
alone, the only question a scientific claim really needs answered: \emph{are these the bytes that
were named?} The three mechanisms they do provide sit entirely on the minting-and-resolution side of
our list. Integrity was never in scope --- and, to be fair to them, was never promised. So of the
four acts of faith, an extrinsic identifier asks you to make all four, and hands you the means to
check none.

\takeaway{Classical \textsc{pid} systems offer generation, assignment, and retrieval --- but no
\emph{verification}. Integrity was never in scope.}

This is not an oversight; it follows from what those systems were built for. A DOI must be able to
name a journal article, a museum specimen, a person --- things whose \enquote{bytes} may not exist at
all, and for which an organization really is the only possible guarantor of uniqueness and
persistence. That generality is bought at a price, and the price is precisely the property research
software needs most. Set the requirements out plainly --- identifiers must be \emph{gratis}, because
there are billions of objects to name; they must guarantee \emph{integrity}, because reproducibility
rests on it; and they must need \emph{no middle man}, because the practice has to outlive any one
institution --- and the survey behind Table~\ref{tab:mechanisms} could find no existing system
offering the last two together~\autocite{swhipres2018}. The Merkle construction of
Chapter~\ref{ch:foundations} is what finally does, and it does so by striking two of the four acts of
faith off the list outright: minting needs no issuer, because the name is computed rather than
granted; and integrity needs no guarantor, because the name \emph{is} the check. What remains ---
resolution and availability --- is an archive's job, and, being a job about \emph{keeping} rather
than \emph{meaning}, it is one you can hedge: mirror it, replicate it, audit it from the outside.

Abstractions are easy to nod along to, so make that empty row concrete. A dataset is deposited in a
repository, given a DOI, and cited in a dozen papers. Some time later --- for good reasons or bad; a
privacy takedown, a corrected outlier, an inconvenient record quietly dropped --- rows are removed
and the deposit is updated in place. The DOI still resolves. The landing page still looks right. The
title, the authors, the citation count: all unchanged. Every one of those dozen papers still points
at \enquote{the data}, and not one of them now points at the data its results were computed from.

Notice that nothing has been violated. No rule was broken and no system failed; the registry is
behaving exactly as designed, because the identifier layer never promised otherwise. That is the
whole point: \emph{with only a DOI, there is no way to detect the change at all.} You cannot tell,
from the identifier, whether the object behind it is still the one the paper meant --- and neither
can the reviewer, the reader, or the author herself five years later. The redaction is not so much
hidden as \emph{unknowable}.

Now give the same dataset a content-derived identifier, and the edit stops being a matter of trust
altogether: the redacted dataset simply has a different hash, and anyone who recomputes it sees the
mismatch at once --- offline, with no registry to consult and no one's permission to ask. The change
may of course be entirely legitimate. What changes is that it becomes \emph{visible}, and so can be
discussed. An intrinsic identifier does not prevent redaction; it makes redaction detectable, which
is all a scientific record can reasonably ask of one.

\takeaway{A DOI cannot tell you the data were altered. A content hash cannot help but tell you.}

Run the experiment once more, in the other direction, on something of your own. Pick the single
dataset or program your group would least like to lose --- the one a decade of results quietly rests
on. Suppose that five years from now it is gone; or, worse, still sitting exactly where it always
sat, but subtly not what it was. What would you need, \emph{today}, to be able to tell the difference
\emph{then}?

Not a URL, which will have rotted. Not a folder of files, which cannot testify about itself. What you
need is a single name that pins every file in the thing at once --- and that is exactly what a
\emph{directory} identifier gives you. As Chapter~\ref{ch:foundations} showed, the hash of a
directory node is computed from the hashes of its entries, which are computed from theirs, all the
way down to the bytes of every file; one short string therefore stands for an entire tree, and
nothing can be added, removed, renamed, or altered anywhere beneath it without changing that string.
Archive the thing, write down that one identifier, and \enquote{is this still what it was?} becomes a
computation instead of an argument.

You can go one further step for almost nothing. Keep a \emph{manifest}: a short plain-text file
listing, for the handful of artifacts your group genuinely depends on, what each one is and the
identifier that pins it. Commit it beside your code, publish it with your data-management plan,
attach it to the thesis. It is a few lines of housekeeping, and it converts \enquote{we are fairly
sure this is the right version} into something a reader, a reviewer, or a successor can check for
themselves years from now --- without having to reach you, and without having to trust you.

\takeaway{One directory identifier pins a whole tree; a short manifest of them pins everything your
group cannot afford to lose.}

\section{The SWHID}

The intrinsic identifier for source code is the \textsc{swhid} --- the \emph{Software Hash
Identifier} --- and it is simply the name of a node in the Merkle DAG of the previous chapter. In
its core form it reads \swhid{swh:1:\textit{type}:\textit{hash}}, where the type is one of
\texttt{cnt} (a file's content), \texttt{dir} (a directory), \texttt{rev} (a revision, i.e.\ a
commit), \texttt{rel} (a release), or \texttt{snp} (a snapshot of a whole repository). Thus
\swhid{swh:1:cnt:94a9ed02\ldots} \emph{is} the text of the GPL\,v3 license, wherever it appears,
and \swhid{swh:1:rel:22ece559\ldots} is release 2.3.0 of the Darktable project --- not a record
pointing \emph{at} them, but a name computed \emph{from} them, that you can recompute yourself.

Identification, though, is not location, and the \textsc{swhid} keeps the two cleanly apart. When
you do want to point a reader into the archive --- to say not merely \emph{which} code but
\emph{where it came from, and in what context} --- you add qualifiers: \texttt{origin} (the URL the
code was harvested from), \texttt{visit} (which archived crawl), \texttt{anchor} (the node the
\texttt{path} is rooted at), \texttt{path}, and \texttt{lines}. The core name still identifies; the
qualifiers merely locate. Figure~\ref{fig:swhid-anatomy} lays out a fully qualified
\textsc{swhid} with every part labelled; Appendix~\ref{app:swhid-syntax} gives the full grammar.

A fully qualified \textsc{swhid} is precise but long --- it can run to several lines, and, dropped
raw into a sentence, it overflows the page. The remedy is the oldest trick in hypertext: show the
reader the short, readable \emph{core} identifier, and carry the full qualified one --- resolver
prefix and all --- inside the \emph{link}. Hover, and the browser reveals the full target; click,
and the archive opens the exact lines. You keep readability in the narrative and full functionality
on the click. So this very sentence can name the core mapping routine of the Parmap library by a
core \textsc{swhid} you can actually read,
\swhref{swh:1:cnt:43a6b232768017b03da934ba22d9cc3f2726a6c5}{swh:1:cnt:43a6b232768017b03da934ba22d9cc3f2726a6c5;origin=https://github.com/rdicosmo/parmap;anchor=swh:1:rel:373e2604d96de4ab1d505190b654c5c4045db773;path=/src/parmap.ml;lines=192-228},
while the link behind it carries the full qualified identifier and opens lines 192--228 of
\texttt{src/parmap.ml} in the archive. In \LaTeX{} this is one \texttt{\textbackslash href} macro;
Chapter~\ref{ch:ardc} shows how \texttt{biblatex-software} does the same automatically in a
bibliography, through a single configuration switch.

None of this is a private convention. The \textsc{swhid} is an international standard, ISO/IEC
18670:2025, recognized by SPDX, registered with IANA as the \texttt{swh:} URI scheme, and recorded
as Wikidata property P6138~\autocite{isoiec18670}. And it is not theoretical for this very note: as
the Colophon explains, this document is named and verified by its own \textsc{swhid} --- which is
also exactly why it cannot contain that identifier inside itself.

\begin{callout}{Getting a SWHID: retrieve one, or compute one}
\small Two different needs, and it is worth not confusing them. If the code is \emph{already in the
archive} and you only want its \textsc{swhid}, nothing need be installed: the archive's red \emph{Permalinks}
tab hands you a fully qualified \textsc{swhid} for whatever file, directory, or line range you are
looking at, and the \emph{updateswh} browser extension will archive a GitHub/GitLab/Bitbucket
repository and then do the same. But that is the archive \emph{reporting} an identifier it computed
--- not a check of your own bytes. To \emph{compute} a \textsc{swhid} from local files, or to \emph{verify} that
a claimed one still matches them --- offline, with no archive and no registry in the loop --- you need
a command-line tool: \textbf{Python} (\texttt{pip install 'swh.model[cli]'}, the \texttt{swh identify}
command) or the \textbf{Rust reference tool} used in the labs below (\texttt{cargo install swhid}).
Appendix~\ref{app:swhid-tools} sorts the ecosystem by exactly this question --- what computes a
\textsc{swhid}, and what is built for something else. \emph{Installing one is your Lab~0}; for a class,
a shared container avoids per-machine setup.
\end{callout}

\begin{handson}[Hands-on: watch the name follow the bytes]
\labmeta{$\sim$10 min \textperiodcentered{} needs any SWHID tool (Appendix~\ref{app:swhid-tools}) \textperiodcentered{} in-class, offline}
Install the Rust reference implementation of the \textsc{swhid} standard --- \texttt{swhid-rs},
released under the MIT license --- with \texttt{cargo install swhid}.\footnote{\url{https://github.com/swhid/swhid-rs}.
A pre-built binary for your platform is also available from the project's releases page. The
reference implementation tracks ISO/IEC~18670 directly; Appendix~\ref{app:swhid-tools} surveys
the other tools.}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
  \item Compute a content \textsc{swhid} for any file: \texttt{swhid content -{}-file file.c}.
  \item Change a single byte and recompute --- the identifier changes completely.
  \item Restore the byte; the original identifier returns, to the character. No registry was ever
        consulted, and no authority was asked --- the name simply follows the bytes.
\end{enumerate}
You can confirm the round trip in one step with \texttt{swhid verify file.c swh:1:cnt:\textellipsis}:
it exits cleanly only when the bytes still match the name.
\end{handson}

\begin{handson}[Hands-on: a readable link]
\labmeta{$\sim$10 min \textperiodcentered{} needs \LaTeX{} and a browser \textperiodcentered{} in-class}
Reproduce the split above on a routine of your own: put the full qualified \textsc{swhid}
(resolver prefix, \texttt{origin}, \texttt{path}, \texttt{lines}) in the \emph{target} of a
\texttt{\textbackslash href}, and show only the core \textsc{swhid} as the visible text; click to
confirm it opens exactly those lines. Rather than hand-build the \texttt{\textbackslash href} each

\end{handson}

We now have what the rest of these notes need: an intrinsic identifier we can trust --- one that
identifies code exactly, that anyone can verify without asking permission, and that, when we choose,
can also tell us where the code lived. The remaining chapters are about putting that identifier to
work: archiving the
code, referencing it at the right granularity, describing it, and citing it. That practice is
\textsc{ardc}, and it is next.
