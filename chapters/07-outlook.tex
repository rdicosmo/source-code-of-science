sovereignty are others.}

\section{It is already happening}

None of this is hypothetical. Journals and platforms across disciplines have wired Software Heritage
into how they work: the HAL national repository offers a curated software-deposit workflow; IPOL
publishes image-processing papers with runnable code and a \textsc{swhid}; \emph{eLife} archives the code
behind every article automatically; \emph{swMATH} references mathematical software in the archive;
and Episciences journals such as JTCAM ask authors to cite their software with
\texttt{biblatex-software} (itself shipped in the ACM article class since
2022)~\autocite{SwhCACM2018}. The connections keep multiplying: Episciences now links each published
article to its code through a \textsc{swhid}~\autocite{episciences2025swh}; \emph{Zenodo} forwards
its software deposits to the archive (Chapter~\ref{ch:ardc}); and code-publishing venues such as
Dagstuhl's \emph{DROPS} interconnect the same way.\footnote{\url{https://drops.dagstuhl.de/}.} Papers
themselves now carry not one identifier but many, pinpointing their code passages with qualified
\textsc{swhid}s throughout the text~\autocite{spinellis2026hotspots}.

And the stakes are real, not niche. French law took the measure of this directly: article~163 of
the 2022 \enquote{3DS} law required the government to report to Parliament, within eighteen months,
on the software produced by public research --- a mandate answered in 2023 by the \emph{Rapport
Boulet}~\autocite{boulet2023etatdeslieux,LoiArticle163_2022}. Built on 1\,331 detailed responses,
it found long-lived, widely-used, high-impact work: half of the software reported was more than nine
years old, over a third (36\%) had more than a hundred users, nearly two-thirds (62\%) reported
impact beyond academia, and almost a quarter (23\%) already fed technology transfer --- and it was
overwhelmingly open, with only about a tenth kept proprietary. This is not a fringe of scientific
practice; it is a load-bearing part of it, and it has been going uncared-for.

That survey, though, was deliberately narrow: a stock-take of \emph{production and valorization},
answering a precise legal question, not an examination of the code itself. Point the same lens at the
actual corpus --- the software French public research has archived in Software Heritage, some
32\,694 projects carrying contributions from the country's universities and research organizations
--- and a sharper picture emerges (Figure~\ref{fig:fr-corpus}). Only about a fifth of those projects
carry any license at all, so by default most of them cannot legally be reused; and roughly seven in
ten live on a single commercial platform, \texttt{github.com}, a concentration that is itself a
systemic risk. The law asked \emph{how much}; the archive can show \emph{in what state} --- and the
state is exactly what the rest of these notes are written to improve.

Recognition, not just adoption, has begun to follow. In 2022 the French ministry created a standing
national award for free and open-source research software --- the \emph{Prix science ouverte du
logiciel libre de la recherche} --- and its inaugural edition drew 129 nominations, from which the
jury singled out four winners and six honourable mentions
(\emph{accessit})~\autocite{ResearchSoftwareAward2023}. The roll of winners shows exactly the breadth
these notes keep asserting, made concrete: the proof assistant \emph{Coq} from computer
science,\footnote{\url{https://coq.inria.fr/}} \emph{scikit-learn} from machine
learning,\footnote{\url{https://scikit-learn.org/}} \emph{Faust} from computer music and
audio,\footnote{\url{https://faust.grame.fr/}} and \emph{Gammapy} from gamma-ray
astronomy\footnote{\url{https://gammapy.org/}} --- four fields, one practice. The prize is now
annual; its second edition, in 2023, drew 66 projects~\autocite{PrixSwLibre2023}. The reasoning
behind it --- why a country should reward research software at all, and how a jury should judge it
--- has itself been written up as a reusable blueprint for other nations to
copy~\autocite{ResearchSoftwareAward2023}. Career credit for the people who write software, the very
thing this chapter's call to action will ask of institutions, is here already national policy.

\takeaway{Coq, scikit-learn, Faust, Gammapy: a national award whose winners span four disciplines ---
software matters in every field, made concrete.}

And we are no longer measuring this by hand. The French Open Science Monitor we met in
Chapter~\ref{ch:why} (Figure~\ref{fig:os-monitor}) does not count software paper by paper: it mines
the open-access literature automatically, with an improved software-mention extractor in the
\emph{Softcite} lineage --- the same dataset the Chan Zuckerberg Initiative used to train the model
that surfaced the hidden footprint of open-source software in the biomedical
literature~\autocite{czi_oss_2022}. Building it on open technologies was always a bet that others
could run it too --- and the bet is paying off. Under a joint UNESCO and French impulse, the method
is being generalized into an \textbf{Open Science Monitoring Framework}: a shared, reproducible
recipe for monitoring open science with open tools, rather than with the proprietary bibliometric
databases the task has long depended on~\autocite{osmf_unesco_2023}. The software strand of that
framework has its own dedicated infrastructure --- \textbf{SoFAIR} --- which identifies software
mentions across the scholarly literature and links each one back to the archived code in Software
Heritage, closing the loop between a citation in a paper and a verifiable
\textsc{swhid}~\autocite{sofair2023}. So the very figure that opened our case is itself on the move:
a national dashboard becoming a worldwide instrument, and what a community can finally measure, it
can finally choose to value.

\section{The policy landscape}\label{sec:policy-backs}

The direction of travel, at every level, is now unambiguous. The \textbf{UNESCO Recommendation on
Open Science} (2021) calls for source code to be released openly, and for open-science
infrastructures to be \enquote{organized and financed upon an essentially not-for-profit and
long-term vision}~\autocite{UNESCO2021OpenScience}. The European \textbf{EOSC SIRS report} (2020) is
more specific still: \emph{archive in Software Heritage, use the SWHID}, and treat open source as the
default for research software, with deviations to be justified~\autocite{SIRSReport2020}. The
\textbf{Paris Call on Software Source Code} (2019) had already urged the world to \enquote{recognise
software source code as a fundamental enabler in all aspects of human endeavour} and to build shared
infrastructures to preserve it~\autocite{ParisCall2019}. And at the national level the message is the
same: France's second \textbf{National Plan for Open Science} names Software Heritage and the
\textsc{swhid} explicitly, the country's research-infrastructure roadmap now lists the archive, and a
standing College for research software carries the agenda forward~\autocite{PNSO2_2021,osec_2022}.
Strip away the acronyms and a single instruction remains: archive in Software Heritage, and identify
with a \textsc{swhid}.

A fair objection is that a report, however specific, is still just a report --- a recommendation can
sit on a shelf. This one did not. In 2021 the EOSC Association stood up a \emph{Task Force on
Infrastructures for Quality Research Software}, chartered to foster the tools and services that let
researchers archive, reference, describe, share, and reuse their software, to raise its quality
technically and organizationally, and to win recognition for the people who build and maintain
it~\autocite{EOSC_TaskForce2021}. And in 2022 the \textbf{FAIRCORE4EOSC} project began building it:
one of its work packages implements the very architecture the SIRS report had sketched, wiring
Software Heritage to the repositories, publishers, and aggregators around it through concrete
connectors --- InvenioRDM, Episciences, Dagstuhl, swMATH, Zenodo, OpenAIRE~\autocite{FAIRCORE4EOSC2022}.
The line runs 2020 report, 2021 task force, 2022 implementation: not a recommendation gathering dust,
but a blueprint being wired into the European research infrastructure.

There is, though, a difference in \emph{kind} that France draws more sharply than anyone, and
it is worth making explicit.\takeaway{Soft policy urges; France's 2016 \emph{loi Lemaire} and
its 2021 Prime-Ministerial directive \emph{compel} open source by default.} Everything cited so
far --- recommendations, plans, roadmaps, funding conditions --- is \emph{soft} policy: it urges
and it nudges, and a determined laggard can still decline. France has also \emph{legislated}. The
2016 \emph{Law for a Digital Republic} --- the \enquote{loi Lemaire} --- made the source code of
software both \emph{produced by} and \emph{received by} the administration an administrative
document, open by default and communicable on request~\autocite{LoiLemaire2016}. A 2021 directive
of the Prime Minister then turned the principle into machinery: it opens public-administration
data and source code by default, stands up a dedicated \enquote{open-source software mission}, and
installs in each ministry a network of data, algorithm and source-code officers --- the
\textsc{amdac} --- charged with carrying the obligation out~\autocite{PMdirective2021}. The effect
is not a pledge but a pipeline: code written for the French public administration now flows into
Software Heritage through \texttt{code.gouv.fr}, on the authority of a statute rather than a
recommendation.\footnote{The public-sector codebase is published and archived through
\url{https://code.gouv.fr/} (formerly \texttt{code.etalab.gouv.fr}); see
\url{https://www.etalab.gouv.fr/code-etalab-gouv-fr-les-codes-sources-de-logiciels-publies-par-des-organismes-publics}.}
The distinction is not pedantry: a recommendation tells you what good practice looks like; a law
makes its absence a fault.

Step back from France, though, and the same instruction reappears in jurisdiction after jurisdiction
--- which is what turns a national success story into a landscape. At the European level it predates
all of them: already in 2018 the Commission formally called on every Member State to adopt a national
action plan \enquote{reinforcing the preservation and re-use of scientific information} ---
publications, datasets, and other research outputs, source code among
them~\autocite{ECRec2018_790}. France's plan is, in this light, one Member State doing what the
Commission asked; others are doing the same. Nor does the agreement stop at Europe's edge: in the
United States, NASA's open science-information policy makes the software behind agency-funded research
an expected, openly available output in its own right, alongside the data and the
papers~\autocite{NASA_OSI_2022}.

Funders, too, have moved beyond France, and tellingly the lever several of them reach for is the one
these notes keep coming back to --- recognition. Germany's Deutsche Forschungsgemeinschaft, the
country's central research funder, added \emph{research software} to its template academic CV in 2022,
so that the code a researcher writes can be listed, and weighed, next to their
publications~\autocite{DFG_CV_2022}. And the funders have begun to act not one agency at a time but
together: Science Europe --- the association of Europe's major public research-funding and
research-performing organizations --- has taken research software into its open-science work through
a dedicated working group, with a software strand at the 2022 Open Science European
Conference~\autocite{osec_2022}. That is a different kind of policy actor from the ones above: not a
ministry issuing an instrument, but the funders themselves, collectively \emph{drafting} the policy
upstream of any published text. The signatures differ, the languages differ, the legal instruments
differ; the instruction does not.

\begin{callout}{A policy we helped shape: the 2019 ``upload filters'' episode}
\small
Policy is not only something that backs us from above --- it is something a community can help write.
When the European Union finalised its \textbf{Copyright Directive} (Directive (EU)~2019/790) in the
spring of 2019, one provision --- the so-called \enquote{upload filters} --- would have obliged
platforms to screen everything their users post against rights-holders'
databases~\autocite{EU_CopyrightDir2019}. Drafted with music and video in mind, it swept up the entire
machinery of modern software development: the forges where code is written, the distribution
platforms that carry it (Maven, PyPI, CRAN, CTAN), and preservation archives such as Software
Heritage. Source code that legitimately reuses or quotes other source code would have been caught in a
filter built for pop songs. After sustained engagement from the open-source and research-software
community --- in which Software Heritage helped raise policymakers' awareness, briefed NGOs, and
opened channels to governments --- an explicit exclusion was obtained for open-source software
developing \emph{and} sharing platforms, and the \enquote{not-for-profit} qualifier that would have
gutted it was struck. The lesson is worth keeping: the same community that asks for policy to protect
its infrastructure can, when it shows up, keep bad policy from breaking it.
\end{callout}

\section{The engine that carries it out: the OSPO}

Policy written at the top does not execute itself. A national plan can name Software Heritage and the
\textsc{swhid}, a funder can ask for an open license and a deposit --- and still nothing changes in a
lab unless someone, locally, turns the principle into the default path. In industry that someone has a
name: the \emph{Open Source Program Office}, or \textsc{ospo} --- a small competence centre that owns
an organization's open-source strategy, its compliance, and its security, and that has by now become
common enough to warrant an annual census of its own~\autocite{lfospo2024,todogroup_ospo}.

Universities are standing up the same thing, adapted to research. A university \textsc{ospo} is the
local execution engine that connects national open-science policy to the daily life of a group: it
makes open-by-default the path of least resistance, and absorbs the license and compliance questions
so that researchers need not; it runs the archiving and citation plumbing --- the
\texttt{CITATION.cff} file, the \textsc{swhid}, the curated deposit --- so that \textsc{ardc}
(Chapter~\ref{ch:ardc}) becomes routine rather than heroic; it trains people in reproducibility and
continuous integration; and it sees to it that software contributions are recognized and counted in
careers. Registries such as OSPO-RADAR (Chapter~\ref{ch:ardc}) are exactly the kind of catalogue such
an office keeps current. In short, the \textsc{ospo} is where the policy of the previous section meets
the practice of the whole of these notes.

\takeaway{Policy needs a local engine: the university OSPO is where open-science mandates become
ordinary research practice.}

\section{The wider mission: resilience, sovereignty, transparent AI}

Open science is only one of the things this archive makes possible, and stepping back reveals why it
matters beyond the library. The same uniform record of the world's source code is the foundation for
several urgent agendas at once. It underpins \emph{software supply-chain traceability}: an
intrinsic identifier is exactly what a software bill of materials (an \textsc{sbom}) needs, and what
regulators reaching for the EU Cyber Resilience Act will
require~\autocite{EU_CRA_2024,barais2025swhidcyber}. And the scale of that dependency problem is not
guesswork: industry audits find open-source components in \(96\)--\(97\%\) of the commercial codebases
they examine, making up around three-quarters (some \(77\%\)) of the code, with an average of over
five hundred distinct open-source components in a single
application~\autocite{ossra2024,ossra2025}. Modern software is, by an overwhelming margin, assembled
rather than written --- and every one of those components can move, change, or quietly disappear, so
preserving the \emph{dependency graph}, not merely the top-level repository, is the whole of the
supply-chain argument. It underpins
\emph{transparent AI}: the very large code corpora that train today's models --- The Stack, on which
StarCoder is built --- are drawn from Software Heritage, and provenance for that data is becoming a
legal as well as an ethical demand~\autocite{StarCoder2_2024}. And it underpins \emph{resilience}:
because the archive holds the code with its history, a build system can fall back to it when the
upstream platform fails. Guix has done exactly this since 2019 --- when a package vanishes from its
registry, the build continues from Software Heritage~\autocite{dicosmo_tpdl_2022,SWHecosystems2023}.
The point is not academic. If GitHub or PyPI disappeared tomorrow, much of the digital world would
not merely slow down --- it would stop.

This reframes the whole enterprise. The goal is not to \emph{own} the infrastructure but to ensure
that nothing essential can vanish --- a shared, open, non-rival archive that benefits all while
leaving each free to build on it. That is digital sovereignty rightly understood: not autarky, but
\emph{resilience}.

\section{A fair question: what about Software Heritage itself?}

These notes have argued that platforms fail and that we should plan for it --- so they must turn the same
lens on the archive they recommend. Software Heritage is a non-profit initiative launched in 2016,
hosted by Inria and run as an international, multi-stakeholder effort under an agreement with UNESCO;
it is funded by a range of institutional members and sponsors rather than by any single company or
state, with foundational support from bodies such as the Alfred P.\ Sloan and NLnet foundations. Its
answer to its own single-point-of-failure risk is a \emph{network of independent mirrors} --- full
copies of the archive held by partner institutions, so the corpus survives the loss of any one
operator. It is the same resilience-by-redundancy the archive offers your build chain, turned on
itself. For an editor or a funder weighing a ten-year commitment, that --- not a metaphor --- is the
due-diligence answer.

It is equally worth being clear about the \emph{limits}. A \textsc{swhid} secures the \emph{identity}
and \emph{availability} of source code; it does not make a build deterministic
(Chapter~\ref{ch:repro}), and it cannot open code that was never public. Adoption, though real and
growing, is still offered as one option among several at most venues rather than a default. The
claim of these notes is not that the work is finished --- it is that the infrastructure, the standard,
and the practice are now ready to be made the default.

\section{Call to action}

Which brings us, finally, back to you. The tools are built, the policy is written, the adoption is
under way; the one thing still missing is the habit. So make it, at three scales.

\emph{As a researcher:} archive the code behind your next result in Software Heritage, reference it
with a \textsc{swhid} at the granularity your claim needs, describe it, and cite it. That is the
whole of \textsc{ardc}, and you can do it this week.

\emph{As a reviewer, an editor, or a member of an artifact-evaluation committee:} ask authors for
archival proof --- a \textsc{swhid} --- rather than a fragile URL. It is one small habit, but applied
every time, it changes the norms of a whole community.

\emph{As an institution or a funder:} write Software Heritage into your open-science policy, become a
member or run a mirror, and --- not least --- count high-quality software contributions in the
careers of the people who make them.

\begin{callout}{Ready to paste: a grant condition and a policy line}
\small \textbf{Grant condition} (it costs the grantee nothing --- archival is free):
\begin{quote}\itshape
Source code produced with this grant must be released under an open license and archived in Software
Heritage, and the resulting \textsc{swhid} reported in the project's outputs. (Further documentation,
testing, and packaging are encouraged but not required.)
\end{quote}
\textbf{Open-science / sovereignty policy line:}
\begin{quote}\itshape
We adopt intrinsic, standard identifiers (\textsc{swhid}, ISO/IEC~18670) and a shared, non-profit
archive (Software Heritage) for research software, ensuring that publicly funded code remains
verifiable, citable, and recoverable independently of any commercial platform.
\end{quote}
A funder can audit compliance across a whole portfolio with one HAL-API call
(Chapter~\ref{ch:ardc}'s \texttt{collCode\_s} query), no per-project chasing required.
\end{callout}

\subsection{A working agenda for funders and policymakers}

The three scales above are things one person can act on tomorrow. But the deepest leverage sits one
level up --- with those who write the calls, set the rules, and sign the cheques --- and there the
choices are systemic, not personal. Four of them, in particular, decide whether the practice this
note describes becomes the default or stays a curiosity. They are worth stating plainly, as an
agenda.

\emph{First, default to open.} Software produced with public money should be open source as a matter
of course, and any departure should have to be justified --- not the reverse. This is the European
line (\S\ref{sec:policy-backs}) taken to its conclusion: where the EOSC SIRS report asks that
deviations be \enquote{properly motivated}~\autocite{SIRSReport2020}, a funder can go one step
further and make open the default, putting the burden of proof squarely on the exception.
Closed-by-default is the costly choice; it is the one that should have to argue for itself.

\emph{Second, avoid balkanization.} The instinct, once a need is recognized, is for each institution
to stand up its own archive, its own identifiers, its own registry. That way lies a patchwork of
silos that do not interoperate and that each carry the full cost of survival alone. The alternative
is to build on shared, open, non-profit infrastructure --- Software Heritage for the source code
itself --- so that effort accumulates instead of fragmenting.

\emph{Third, fund the commons as a commons.} A mutualized infrastructure is not free because it is
shared; it needs recurrent, predictable funding rather than one-off project grants, and it must be
evaluated as the long-lived service it is. Above all it must be funded for what it predominantly
\emph{is}: not a server bill, but the people who curate, maintain, and run it. The human component is
the larger part of the cost, and the first to be cut when infrastructure is mistaken for hardware.

\emph{Fourth, incentivize intelligently.} If we want researchers to write, share, and maintain good
software, the careers of the people who do it must reward exactly that --- high-quality
contributions, counted as first-class scholarly output. The temptation is to reach instead for a
number (papers, citations, downloads) and let it stand in for worth. Goodhart's law warns where that
ends: \enquote{when a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good
measure}~\autocite{strathern1997improving}. Keep a human in the loop, and judge contributions on
their merits rather than on a metric that will, predictably, be gamed.

\begin{callout}{A four-point agenda for research-software policy}
\small For those who design the rules, not just follow them:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Default to open.} Publicly funded research software is open source by default;
        exceptions \emph{must} be justified.
  \item \textbf{Avoid balkanization.} Build on common, shared, open, non-profit infrastructure
        (Software Heritage), not per-institution silos.
  \item \textbf{Fund the commons as a commons.} Recurrent funding and proper service evaluation,
        recognizing that the human component is the predominant cost.
  \item \textbf{Incentivize intelligently.} Count quality contributions in careers; avoid purely
        numerical indicators; keep a human in the loop.
\end{enumerate}
\end{callout}

Software source code is a precious, unique form of knowledge, and for too long we have treated it as
an afterthought. We no longer have to. The argument is settled and the machinery is ready; what is
needed now is simply that we use it. Record your code, share it, and value
it~\autocite{NatureSD2026}. Start with your next paper.
