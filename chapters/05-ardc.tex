\chapter{ARDC in practice}
\label{ch:ardc}

\begin{objectives}\small
\begin{itemize}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
  \item archive your code by the channel that fits your situation (forge or tarball);
  \item reference it at the granularity a claim needs, with a qualified \textsc{swhid};
  \item describe it (\texttt{codemeta.json}, \texttt{CITATION.cff}, an \textsc{spdx} licence)
        and cite it with \texttt{biblatex-software}.
\end{itemize}
\end{objectives}

We now have everything we need to do the work: an archive that keeps source code for the long
term, and an intrinsic identifier that names exactly the code we mean. This chapter is the
operational heart of these notes. It turns the principle ``treat software as a first-class output''
into four concrete verbs you can apply to a repository of your choice --- your own code, a library
you depend on, a result you want to cite. The verbs are \textbf{A}rchive, \textbf{R}eference,
\textbf{D}escribe, and \textbf{C}ite~\&~credit; together they spell \textsc{ardc}, and each ends
with a hands-on lab.

\takeaway{Archive once; then reference at the granularity your claim needs.}

\section{Archive}

\begin{callout}{If you only do one thing}
Go to \url{https://save.softwareheritage.org}, paste your repository's clone URL, and submit. Your
code, with its history, is now preserved and has a permanent \textsc{swhid}. Everything else in this
chapter is worthwhile polish on top of that one irreversible win.
\end{callout}

Recall the one hard requirement from the CODE roadmap: \emph{publish and archive}. Publishing on a
forge you already do. Archiving means depositing the code, with its history, into Software
Heritage, and there are four ways in, in rising order of automation~\autocite{SwhCACM2018}.

The simplest is \emph{Save Code Now}: go to \url{https://save.softwareheritage.org}, paste the
clone URL of a public repository, and the archive will visit it --- Git, Mercurial, Subversion, no
matter. One step better is the \emph{updateswh} browser extension
(Figure~\ref{fig:updateswh}), which puts a single ``archive this'' button on the repository pages of
GitHub, GitLab, and Bitbucket.\footnote{Install links and documentation:
\url{https://www.softwareheritage.org/browser-extensions/}; source at
\url{https://github.com/rdicosmo/updateswh}.} Better still, for code you maintain, is a
\emph{webhook}: wire your forge so that every release triggers archival automatically, and you
never have to remember again. And there is a fourth channel, the
\textsc{sword}-based \emph{deposit} (\url{https://deposit.softwareheritage.org}) --- but it is
\emph{not} a general path: it is reserved for trusted partners and registered organizations ---
a journal, an institution, or HAL (France's national open-access repository) --- who hold
credentials to push a tarball with its metadata
straight into the archive. An ordinary researcher does not have those credentials, and does not
need them: the easy paths below cover exactly the case the deposit was built to handle.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=.6\linewidth]{figures/updateswh-extension.png}
  \caption[The updateswh browser extension]{The \emph{updateswh} browser extension on a repository
  page: its button shows the archival status at a glance --- already in Software Heritage, or one
  click away via \emph{Save Code Now}.}
  \label{fig:updateswh}
\end{figure}

\subsection*{When all you have is a tarball}

The three public channels above all assume one thing: that your code lives in a forge, behind a
clone URL. \emph{And yet} a great deal of research software does not --- it is a \texttt{.zip} or
\texttt{.tar.gz} attached to a paper, a snapshot with no public Git remote. For that case, two
paths are open to you, and neither needs partner credentials.

The easiest, by far, is to go through \emph{Zenodo}. Since the Zenodo--Software Heritage
integration launched in late 2024, software you deposit on Zenodo is \emph{automatically} sent on
to Software Heritage, provided the files are publicly accessible; Software Heritage computes its
\textsc{swhid}, and Zenodo links that \textsc{swhid} to the record's \textsc{doi} and shows both on
the landing page~\autocite{zenodoswh2024}. So you upload your archive once, and walk away with
\emph{both} identifiers --- the \textsc{doi} for discoverability in the scholarly literature, and
the \textsc{swhid} for the verifiable identity of the exact bytes. It is the same division of
labour we have insisted on throughout: \emph{a \textsc{doi} for findability, a \textsc{swhid} for
integrity} --- and here you get both, for the price of one upload, with no forge required.

The second path stays inside Software Heritage: \emph{Save Code Now} can also ingest a \texttt{.zip}
or tarball directly, rather than a clone URL. This is open to any registered account (the account is
free; an anonymous visitor can still submit only a version-control URL), but a tarball deposit enters
a \emph{review queue} and is archived only once a moderator has approved it --- and that queue is not
always cleared quickly enough for time-sensitive needs, such as a journal that wants a paper's
artifacts archived as soon as it is accepted. Holders of the \emph{ambassador} role bypass the queue,
their tarball deposits being ingested directly; but the ambassador role is far more than a sign-up.
It is a vetted community programme --- ambassadors are volunteers who actively promote and support
Software Heritage in their field, and one joins by applying to the
programme.\footnote{Software Heritage Ambassador programme:
\url{https://www.softwareheritage.org/ambassadors/}.} So for a one-off the Zenodo route above is
simpler and immediate; for archiving tarballs regularly --- shepherding a journal's artifacts, say
--- the ambassador route is worth pursuing.

However the code got in, the result is the same: it is now kept, and it now has a \textsc{swhid}. The official
Software Heritage HOWTO walks through the whole recipe, and its three steps map directly onto the
rest of this chapter:

\begin{tcolorbox}[colback=swhbg!35,colframe=swhdark,fonttitle=\bfseries\sffamily,
  title={The official HOWTO\footnotemark}, sharp corners=downhill, boxrule=0.4pt,
  left=2.2mm,right=2.2mm,top=1mm,bottom=1mm]
\small
\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Prepare} your public repository: add a \texttt{README}, an \texttt{AUTHORS} file,
        and licence information (a \texttt{LICENSE} file at the root, or a \texttt{LICENSES/}
        directory).
  \item \textbf{Save} your code in Software Heritage: manually with the \emph{updateswh} extension;
        manually via \emph{Save Code Now} (pick the version-control system, enter the repository URL,
        click submit); or automatically through the API.
  \item \textbf{Reference} your code: get the \textsc{swhid} of a full directory, or of a precise
        code fragment.
\end{enumerate}
\end{tcolorbox}
\footnotetext{\emph{How to archive and reference your code}, \url{https://www.softwareheritage.org/how-to-archive-reference-code/}.}

\begin{handson}[Hands-on: archive any repository, and automate it]
\labmeta{$\sim$15 min $+$ archival wait \textperiodcentered{} needs a forge account \textperiodcentered{} take-home (archival is asynchronous)}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
  \item Pick a repository worth keeping --- your own, or one you depend on. Archive it with the
        \textbf{updateswh} extension or at \url{https://save.softwareheritage.org}; wait for the
        visit to reach status \texttt{full}.
  \item Then make it automatic: add a release \emph{webhook} (GitHub, GitLab, Gitea, Bitbucket,
        SourceForge) so each new tag is archived without your lifting a finger.
\end{enumerate}
\end{handson}

\begin{callout}{Over time, and when it does not work}
For a \emph{maintained} project, make archival a habit, not an event: the release webhook above means
every tagged version is preserved as you go, and keeping \texttt{codemeta.json} and
\texttt{CITATION.cff} updated alongside each release keeps your citations current. A few common
snags: a visit that stays \texttt{pending} or \texttt{partial} usually just needs time (large
histories take a while) --- re-check rather than re-submit; \emph{private} repositories cannot be
archived until they are public; a \emph{monorepo} is archived whole, and you reference the
sub-directory with a \texttt{path} qualifier; and a very large artifact is better archived as its
git repository than as a tarball.
\end{callout}

\section{Reference}

Archiving gives the code an identifier; \emph{referencing} is the craft of choosing \emph{which} one to
quote, and at what granularity. Here the identifiers of Chapter~\ref{ch:identifiers} come into
their own, because a paper rarely depends on ``the repository'' --- it depends on something far
more specific. Match the identifier to the claim~\autocite{cise-2020-doi}:

\begin{description}[leftmargin=!,labelwidth=2.6cm,font=\bfseries\sffamily]
  \item[the project] ``Inria created OCaml and Scikit-learn'' --- name the project (a \texttt{snp},
        or the origin).
  \item[a release] ``2D Voronoi diagrams were introduced in CGAL 3.1.0'' --- name the release
        (\texttt{rel}).
  \item[an exact state] ``this result was produced using the exact commit behind it'' --- name the
        revision (\texttt{rev}).
  \item[a code fragment] ``the core routine \texttt{simplemapper} sits at lines 192--228 of
        \texttt{src/parmap.ml} in release 1.1.1'' --- name the content, with its \texttt{path} and
        \texttt{lines}.
\end{description}

For a paper you will usually want a \emph{qualified} \textsc{swhid}: the core identifier itself,
plus the \texttt{origin}, \texttt{visit}, and \texttt{anchor} qualifiers that let a reader walk
straight to it in the archive and see where it came from. And when no precise version is known ---
when you simply want to point at ``the project, as the archive saw it'' --- an origin-qualified
\textsc{swhid} serves as a Wayback-machine-style pointer into the crawl history.

\begin{callout}{Which \textsc{swhid} to cite? When in doubt, a directory}
\small To reference a version of the code \emph{as a whole}, Software Heritage recommends a full,
qualified \emph{directory} (\texttt{dir}) \textsc{swhid}~\autocite{swhfaq_swhidtype}: print the short
core \texttt{dir} identifier, and carry the full qualified one in the hyperlink behind it --- exactly
the split the \texttt{\textbackslash swhref} helper makes. The core \texttt{dir} identifier is the most
useful one for reproducibility, because it is computed \emph{locally, from the directory's content
alone}: it stays the same no matter which release, commit, or repository that directory belongs to, and
anyone can recompute it from a copy of the files. A \texttt{rev} identifier, by contrast, is tied to
one commit's history, and a bare \texttt{cnt} names only a single file. So prefer the directory, and
add an \texttt{anchor} qualifier linking it to a release or revision, so a reader can still recover the
version and origin from the archive.
\end{callout}

Here is the last rung made concrete --- the \texttt{simplemapper} routine of the Parmap library,
named to the exact line:

\begin{quote}\footnotesize
\begin{verbatim}
let simplemapper init finalize ncores' compute opid al collect =
  flush_all ();
  let ln = Array.length al in
  set_ncores (min ln (max 1 ncores'));
  ...                                  (* src/parmap.ml, lines 192-228 *)
\end{verbatim}
\vspace{-3pt}\noindent named by
\swhref{swh:1:cnt:43a6b232768017b03da934ba22d9cc3f2726a6c5}{swh:1:cnt:43a6b232768017b03da934ba22d9cc3f2726a6c5;origin=https://github.com/rdicosmo/parmap;anchor=swh:1:rel:373e2604d96de4ab1d505190b654c5c4045db773;path=/src/parmap.ml;lines=192-228}
--- the visible text is the short core identifier; the link behind it carries the full qualified
\textsc{swhid} and opens exactly these lines in the archive.
\end{quote}

\begin{handson}[Hands-on: reference code in a paper]
\labmeta{$\sim$15 min \textperiodcentered{} needs an archived repo \textperiodcentered{} in-class once the artifact is archived}
Take a result of your own that rests on code, and build its reference with the Rust reference tool
\texttt{swhid-rs} (\texttt{cargo install swhid}; see Appendix~\ref{app:swhid-tools}). Compute the
\texttt{cnt} \textsc{swhid} of the precise file --- \texttt{swhid content -{}-file analysis.py} ---
and the \texttt{rev} \textsc{swhid} of the exact commit, which is simply \swhid{swh:1:rev:}
prepended to \texttt{git rev-parse HEAD}. Add the \texttt{path} and \texttt{lines} qualifiers by
hand, then paste each qualified \textsc{swhid} into \texttt{https://archive.softwareheritage.org/}
and confirm it highlights exactly the code you meant.
\end{handson}

\section{Describe}

An identifier keeps code findable; \emph{description} makes it understandable and reusable, and it is
where a little effort pays off enormously. Three files do most of the work, and you should add them
in order of growing ambition~\autocite{boettiger2017codemeta}.

Start with a \texttt{README}: the human entry point, answering \emph{what, why, install, run, cite,
licence} in plain words --- ten minutes today, and the single highest-value thing you can do. As a
project grows, add a \texttt{codemeta.json}: a rich, machine-readable description --- authors,
dependencies, funding, archival identifiers --- in a schema.org-based vocabulary that forges and
Software Heritage can read to generate citations automatically. Do not write it by hand; use the
CodeMeta generator (Figure~\ref{fig:codemeta}).\footnote{\url{https://codemeta.github.io/codemeta-generator/};
the CodeMeta vocabulary and crosswalk: \url{https://codemeta.github.io/}.} From the CodeMeta file
you can in turn generate a \texttt{CITATION.cff}, the lightweight format that makes GitHub show a
``Cite this repository'' button~\autocite{druskat2021cff}.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=.96\linewidth]{figures/CodeMetaGeneratorFull.png}
  \caption[The CodeMeta generator]{The CodeMeta generator: a browser form that writes a
  \texttt{codemeta.json} for you, from fields for the software itself, its discoverability and
  citation, its development tools, its run-time environment, and its authors --- no hand-editing of
  JSON-LD.}
  \label{fig:codemeta}
\end{figure}

And do not forget the licence --- the most consequential one-line file in your repository. By the
Berne Convention, code is copyrighted the moment it is written, and the default is \emph{all rights
reserved}: without a licence, nobody may legally reuse your work, however open you meant it to be.
Yet roughly \emph{ninety per cent of public repositories on GitHub carry no licence at all}, and
research code is no exception: in the corpus of French public-research software archived in Software
Heritage, nearly three-quarters of the projects carry no licence information whatsoever
(Chapter~\ref{ch:outlook}). Choose
one --- permissive (MIT, BSD, Apache-2.0) or copyleft (the GPL family) --- declare it
machine-readably with an \textsc{spdx} identifier, and, if you like, automate the bookkeeping with
the REUSE specification. (Resist the temptation to reach for Creative Commons: it was not designed
for software.) The community has distilled all of this into the FAIR Software Five
recommendations.\footnote{\url{https://fair-software.eu/}}

None of this is much work. Declaring a licence is one line at the top of each source file, and a
citable record is a few lines of \textsc{yaml}:
\begin{quote}\footnotesize
\begin{verbatim}
# SPDX-License-Identifier: MIT      (one line, at the top of each file)

# CITATION.cff
cff-version: 1.2.0
title: My Research Tool
authors:
  - family-names: Curie
    given-names: Marie
version: 1.0.0
license: MIT
\end{verbatim}
\end{quote}

\takeaway{About 90\% of public repositories carry no licence --- so by default nobody may legally
reuse them. A one-line \textsc{spdx} tag fixes it.}

To put your software forward as a citable output --- onto your CV, into your institution's portal
and the literature --- one further step helps: reference it in a curated catalogue. In France that
means depositing in HAL, which is wired directly into Software Heritage
(Figure~\ref{fig:hal-swh}); elsewhere, registries such as OSPO-RADAR play the same role.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=.92\linewidth]{figures/hal-swh-overview.png}
  \caption[HAL and Software Heritage]{The HAL\,/\,Software Heritage synergy: a researcher deposits a
  curated record of a piece of software in the HAL open-access portal, while the source code itself
  is archived in Software Heritage --- the two linked by the code's \textsc{swhid}.}
  \label{fig:hal-swh}
\end{figure}

Once your software is in HAL it becomes \emph{queryable} as a first-class output. A single call to
the HAL API lists every piece of software an entity --- a lab, or a person --- has produced over a
span of years; for all software produced under Inria between 2020 and 2024:
\begin{quote}\footnotesize
\begin{verbatim}
https://api.archives-ouvertes.fr/search/?q=*&wt=json
   &fq=docType_s:SOFTWARE&fq=collCode_s:INRIA
   &fq=producedDateY_i:[2020 TO 2024]
\end{verbatim}
\end{quote}
returns well over a hundred records, each with its \textsc{swhid}. Narrow to a single author ---
\texttt{authIdHal\_s:roberto-di-cosmo} --- and you get that researcher's archived software (here the
Parmap and OcamlP3l libraries, each carrying the \textsc{swhid} of its source). The HALtools
front-end turns the same query into a ready-made HTML list for a CV or a lab
page.\footnote{HAL API: \url{https://api.archives-ouvertes.fr/}; HALtools:
\url{https://haltools.archives-ouvertes.fr/}.}

\begin{handson}[Hands-on: describe, and watch the citation appear]
\labmeta{$\sim$20 min $+$ re-archival wait \textperiodcentered{} needs the archived repo \textperiodcentered{} take-home (asynchronous)}
Create a \texttt{codemeta.json} with the CodeMeta Generator, commit it, and re-archive the
repository. Then open the project in the Software Heritage archive and watch the citation panel
populate from your metadata --- and use it to produce the citation you actually want.
\end{handson}

\section{Cite and credit}

Before the mechanics, the principle. The FORCE11 \emph{Software Citation Principles} settled what had
long been contested: software that underpins a result is a legitimate, first-class research product,
to be cited like any article or dataset, and cited with enough metadata --- authors, version, and a
\emph{persistent, verifiable} identifier --- to find and credit the exact code a claim relied
on~\autocite{force11citationprinciples}. That last word, \emph{credit}, is where it gets genuinely
hard. A research code is rarely the work of one pair of hands, and the contribution that matters is
rarely only the code: testing, documentation, design, and the unglamorous labour of long-term
maintenance all earn a place on the author line. \emph{And yet} an attribution mined automatically
from the \texttt{git} history sees only commits, and quietly writes the non-coding contributors out of
the record~\autocite{2020GtCitation}. Crediting a collective --- \enquote{The Givaro group} rather
than a roster of names --- keeps the record honest about a team effort, but at the cost of the
individual recognition on which academic careers still turn. No bibliography style can resolve that
tension; what a good one \emph{can} do is carry the version, the licence, and the verifiable
identifier on every reference, and leave the human judgement of credit to people.

So, finally, the mechanics. Cite software as you would cite anything else in research: in the
bibliography, as a first-class reference. For decades there was no proper way to do this, and software citations were
forced into \texttt{@misc} entries that lost the version, the licence, and any verifiable
identifier~\autocite{2020GtCitation}. The \texttt{biblatex-software} style fixes
that~\autocite{DiCosmoSEN2020}. It adds four entry types --- \texttt{@software} for a project,
\texttt{@softwareversion} for a release, \texttt{@softwaremodule} for a component, and
\texttt{@codefragment} for a precise span of code --- each carrying a \texttt{swhid=} field. The
power comes from \texttt{crossref}: shared metadata (authors, repository, licence) is written
\emph{once} on the parent \texttt{@software} entry, and each version, fragment, or snapshot adds only
what is specific to it. So citing several versions and several fragments of the \emph{same} project
costs almost nothing:

\begin{quote}\footnotesize
\begin{verbatim}
@software{parmap,                       % the project: shared metadata, written once
  title   = {The Parmap library},
  author  = {Di Cosmo, Roberto and Danelutto, Marco},
  url     = {https://github.com/rdicosmo/parmap},
  license = {LGPL-2.0}}

@softwareversion{parmap-1.1.1, crossref={parmap},          % a released VERSION
  version = {1.1.1}, date = {2020},
  swhid   = {swh:1:rel:373e2604d96de4ab1d505190b654c5c4045db773;
             origin=https://github.com/rdicosmo/parmap}}

@codefragment{simplemapper, crossref={parmap-1.1.1},       % a code FRAGMENT
  subtitle = {Core mapping routine},
  swhid    = {swh:1:cnt:43a6b232768017b03da934ba22d9cc3f2726a6c5;
              path=/src/parmap.ml;lines=192-228}}

@softwareversion{parmap-dev, crossref={parmap},            % a whole-repo SNAPSHOT
  version = {dev snapshot 2017-05},
  swhid   = {swh:1:snp:2a6c348c53eb77d458f24c9cbcecaf92e3c45615;
             origin=https://github.com/rdicosmo/parmap}}
\end{verbatim}
\end{quote}

\noindent In the text you then write \texttt{\textbackslash cite\{parmap-1.1.1\}} for the release,
\texttt{\textbackslash cite\{simplemapper\}} for the routine, or \texttt{\textbackslash cite\{parmap-dev\}}
for the archived snapshot --- and the bibliography fills in the inherited authors, repository, and
licence for each, with the right \textsc{swhid} type (\texttt{rel}, \texttt{cnt}, \texttt{snp})
rendered automatically.

\noindent The package is on CTAN and in TeX\,Live, and has shipped in the ACM \texttt{acmart} class
since 2022, so the \textsc{swhid} renders natively in an ACM paper. It also solves, for the
bibliography, the very readability problem of Chapter~\ref{ch:identifiers}: a single configuration
switch, \texttt{shortswhid}, chooses whether the \emph{full} qualified \textsc{swhid} is printed or
only the short \emph{core} one --- and in both cases the complete identifier stays live in the
hyperlink (Figure~\ref{fig:biblatex-swh}).

The payoff is that a software citation becomes \emph{reproducible}: a \textsc{swhid} in the
bibliography resolves to the exact bytes you used, with no dependence on a URL that may not outlive
the paper. These notes' own bibliography is built this way --- the Parmap library, for instance, is
cited as a versioned software entry carrying its \textsc{swhid}, and a routine within it as a code
fragment.\footnote{See the \texttt{biblatex-software} entries~\autocite{parmap-1.1.1,simplemapper}
in the references.}

\takeaway{A \textsc{swhid} in the bibliography is a citation that still resolves when the URL is
gone.}

\begin{handson}[Hands-on: cite software with biblatex-software]
\labmeta{$\sim$20 min \textperiodcentered{} needs \LaTeX{} with biber \textperiodcentered{} in-class}
\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
  \item Write a \texttt{@softwareversion} entry for the code you archived, with its \texttt{swhid=}
        field; add a \texttt{@codefragment} child via \texttt{crossref} for a specific routine.
  \item Produce the variants --- a project-level \texttt{@software}, a \texttt{@softwaremodule} ---
        and compare how each renders.
  \item Drop the entries into an \texttt{acmart} article and confirm the \textsc{swhid} renders in
        the ACM template.
  \item Flip the option both ways --- \texttt{\textbackslash ExecuteBibliographyOptions\{shortswhid=true\}}
        vs \texttt{false} --- and rebuild: watch the printed identifier shrink to its core while the
        link stays whole (Figure~\ref{fig:biblatex-swh}).
\end{enumerate}
\end{handson}

That is \textsc{ardc}: archive the code, reference it precisely, describe it, and cite it --- a
practice you can adopt incrementally, one verb at a time. The remaining question is what all this
rigour \emph{buys} us. The answer, and the sharpest
test of the whole approach, is reproducibility and the evaluation of research artifacts: the
subject of the next chapter.
