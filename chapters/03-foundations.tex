\chapter{Foundations: forges, the archive, and the data model}
\label{ch:foundations}

Before we can name a piece of code, we need somewhere that reliably keeps it. This chapter
builds that ground in three steps: why the places where we \emph{develop} software are not the
places that \emph{preserve} it; what a universal archive of source code is, and how it works;
and the simple, powerful data structure --- the Merkle DAG --- that turns ``keeping the code''
into ``being able to name it exactly,'' which is the subject of the next chapter.

\takeaway{A forge is where code is developed; an archive is where it is kept.}

\section{Forges are not archives}

Where does the source code behind a paper actually live? In practice, in one of three kinds of
place, and it pays to keep them apart. There are \emph{forges} --- collaborative development
platforms such as GitHub, GitLab, and Bitbucket, built for writing software together, with
version control, issues, and pull requests. There are \emph{distribution platforms} --- CTAN,
CRAN, PyPI, Debian --- built to ship ready-to-use packages to users. And there are
\emph{archives}, built for one purpose: to keep things, for the long term. Software Heritage is
an archive. GitHub is not, and was never meant to be.

This is not a quibble. For thirty years researchers have reached for one of two habits, and both
have a hole in them. The older habit, inherited from the 1990s, is to drop a \texttt{.zip} on a
web page or in a document repository, perhaps with a DOI attached: a poor user experience and ---
as the next chapter shows --- a poor identifier. The newer habit, since the 2000s, is to leave
the code in a forge and cite its URL. That is pleasant to use, and offers \emph{no preservation
guarantee whatsoever}~\autocite{SwhCACM2018}.

How weak is that guarantee? The record is brutal, and it is recent. In 2015, Google Code and
Gitorious shut their doors, putting on the order of a \emph{million} repositories at risk and
breaking links across the web of scholarship. In 2019, Bitbucket announced the end of Mercurial
hosting; in July 2020 it deleted more than \emph{250,000} repositories, research software among
them. In 2022, GitLab.com floated a plan to erase any project left inactive for a year. And lest
this seem a disease of commercial platforms only, in 2021 Inria unplugged its own ageing forge
and broke the build chain of the entire OCaml package ecosystem. Earlier still, the hosting
service CodeSpaces ran happily from 2007 until 2014 --- and then, one bad day, was gone, its
data unrecoverable. Seven years of ``everything is fine'' is not preservation.

The deeper reason is that a URL is a promise nobody signed. As the very specification that
defined them concedes, \enquote{there is no general guarantee that a URL\ldots continues to
[point to the same object]}; Tim Berners-Lee's plea that \enquote{cool URIs don't change} carries
its own sad diagnosis --- URIs don't change, \emph{people change them}. Measured rather than
asserted, the rot is fast: studying the URLs cited in \emph{Computer} and the
\emph{Communications of the ACM}, Spinellis found the half-life of a referenced URL to be about
four years~\autocite{spinellis2003decay}, and Lawrence and colleagues had found the same
fragility throughout the scientific literature~\autocite{lawrence2001persistence}. Digital
information decays if left untended --- the ``bit rot'' and ``digital dark age'' that
preservationists have warned of for a generation~\autocite{cerf2011avoiding,anderson2015digital}
--- and source code, with its web of dependencies and its decades-long lives, decays faster than
most. Keeping it is a job in its own right~\autocite{matthews2010framework}, and it is not the
job a forge ever signed up for.

\section{The universal archive}

If forges will not keep our code, something must. Software Heritage is that something: an
international, non-profit, long-term initiative whose mission is to \emph{collect, preserve, and
share} the source code of all publicly available software --- a modern Library of Alexandria for
code, built once and shared by industry, research, culture, and society as a
whole~\autocite{SwhCACM2018,SWHecosystems2023}.

  \caption[Growth of the Software Heritage archive]{The growth of the Software Heritage archive
  over time: by early 2026 it holds the development history of hundreds of millions of projects
  --- the largest corpus of source code ever assembled.}
  \label{fig:archive-growth}
\end{figure}

It fills itself automatically, by a division of labour worth understanding. \emph{Listers} walk
a hosting platform and enumerate the repositories it holds; \emph{loaders} then ingest each
repository --- its files, its directories, and, crucially, its full commit history --- whatever
the version-control system (Git, Mercurial, Subversion) or package format. New listers and
loaders are contributed by a broad community, with support from the Alfred P.\ Sloan and NLnet
foundations. The result, at the time of writing, is hard to overstate: the development history of
more than \emph{28 billion} unique source files from over \emph{430 million} projects, some three
petabytes of deduplicated content, organised as roughly sixty billion nodes joined by close to a
trillion edges (Figure~\ref{fig:archive-growth}). It is, by a wide margin, the largest body of
source code ever gathered in one place.

The interesting question is not how big the archive is, but how it is \emph{organised} --- because
the organisation is what makes it more than a very large backup.

\section{The Merkle DAG data model}

Archiving the world's code so that anyone can \emph{name} a precise piece of it calls for a
particular kind of identifier. We want identifiers that are \emph{intrinsic} --- derived from the
content itself, not handed out by a registry --- and that work not only for single files but for
\emph{tree structures}, the directories-within-directories that are the natural shape of
software. A classical construction gives us exactly this: the \emph{Merkle
tree}~\autocite{merkle1987digital}, which marries a tree to a cryptographic hash function so that
the name of every node is computed from the names of its children, all the way down to the bytes
of each file.

This single idea buys three properties we could not otherwise have together. Such identifiers are
\emph{gratis} --- you can mint billions of them at no cost; they guarantee \emph{integrity} ---
change one byte and the identifier changes, so an object cannot be altered behind its name; and
they need \emph{no middle-man} --- no central authority has to exist for the name to mean
something. Integrity \emph{and} no middle-man, at once, is exactly the combination a registry
cannot give you --- which is why the same construction underpins Git, Nix, IPFS, and the
blockchains.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=.84\linewidth]{figures/swh-merkle-dag.pdf}
  \caption[The Software Heritage Merkle DAG]{The Software Heritage data model: one Merkle directed
  acyclic graph in which file \emph{contents} (\texttt{cnt}) are gathered into \emph{directories}
  (\texttt{dir}), directories into \emph{revisions} (\texttt{rev}, i.e.\ commits), and those into
  \emph{releases} (\texttt{rel}) and \emph{snapshots} (\texttt{snp}). Each node's name is the hash
  of everything beneath it; identical subtrees are stored once.}
  \label{fig:merkle-dag}
\end{figure}

\takeaway{Gratis, integrity, no middle-man --- a Merkle tree delivers all three at once.}

Software Heritage stores \emph{all} the code it has ever collected, with its full history, in
\emph{one} such structure: a single Merkle directed acyclic graph whose nodes are file contents,
directories, revisions, releases, and snapshots (Figure~\ref{fig:merkle-dag})%
~\autocite{ICMS2020,pietri2019graph}. Because every node is named by the hash of everything below
it, identical files and directories --- of which the world's software has a staggering number ---
are stored exactly once, and the archive as a whole becomes, in effect, a global ledger of public
code whose integrity anyone can verify~\autocite{SWHecosystems2023}.

That is the foundation. We now have somewhere to keep code, and a data model in which every
artifact already \emph{has} a name, computed from its content. The next chapter takes that name
seriously --- and shows how it untangles a confusion that has dogged digital identifiers for
decades.
