\chapter{Foundations: forges, the archive, and the data model}
\label{ch:foundations}

\begin{objectives}\small
\begin{itemize}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
  \item distinguish forges, distribution platforms, and archives;
  \item describe what a universal archive of source code collects, and at what scale;
  \item explain the Merkle-DAG idea and the three properties it buys (gratis, integrity,
        no middle-man).
\end{itemize}
\end{objectives}

Before we can identify a piece of code, we need somewhere that reliably keeps it. This chapter
builds that ground in three steps: why the places where we \emph{develop} software are not the
places that \emph{preserve} it; what a universal archive of source code is, and how it works;
and the simple, powerful data structure --- the Merkle DAG --- that turns ``keeping the code''
into ``being able to identify it exactly,'' which is the subject of the next chapter.

\takeaway{A forge is where code is developed; an archive is where it is kept.}

\section{Forges are not archives}

Where does the source code behind a paper actually live? In practice, in one of three kinds of
place, and it pays to keep them apart. There are \emph{forges} --- collaborative development
platforms such as GitHub, GitLab, and Bitbucket, built for writing software together, with
version control, issues, and pull requests. There are \emph{distribution platforms} --- CTAN,
CRAN, PyPI, Debian --- built to ship ready-to-use packages to users. And there are
\emph{archives}, built for one purpose: to keep things, for the long term. Software Heritage is
an archive. GitHub is not, and was never meant to be.

This is not a quibble. For thirty years researchers have reached for one of two habits, and both
have a hole in them. The older habit, inherited from the 1990s, is to drop a \texttt{.zip} on a
web page or in a document repository, perhaps with a DOI attached: a poor user experience and ---
as the next chapter shows --- a poor identifier. The newer habit, since the 2000s, is to leave
the code in a forge and cite its URL. That is pleasant to use, and offers \emph{no preservation
guarantee whatsoever}~\autocite{SwhCACM2018}.

How weak is that guarantee? The record is brutal, and it is recent. In 2015, Google Code and
Gitorious shut their doors, putting on the order of a \emph{million} repositories at risk and
breaking links across the web of scholarship. In 2019, Bitbucket announced the end of Mercurial
hosting; in July 2020 it deleted more than \emph{250,000} repositories, research software among
them. In 2022, GitLab.com floated a plan to erase any project left inactive for a year. And lest
this seem a disease of commercial platforms only, in 2021 Inria unplugged its own ageing forge
and broke the build chain of the entire OCaml package ecosystem. Earlier still, the hosting
service Code Spaces (not today's GitHub Codespaces) ran happily from 2007 until 2014 --- and then, one bad day, was gone, its
data unrecoverable. Seven years of ``everything is fine'' is not preservation.

The deeper reason is that a URL is a promise nobody signed. As the very specification that
defined them concedes, \enquote{there is no general guarantee that a URL\ldots continues to
[point to the same object]}~\autocite{rfc1738}; Tim Berners-Lee's plea that \enquote{cool URIs don't change} carries
its own sad diagnosis --- URIs don't change, \emph{people change them}. Measured rather than
asserted, the rot is fast: studying the URLs cited in \emph{Computer} and the
\emph{Communications of the ACM}, Spinellis found the half-life of a referenced URL to be about
four years~\autocite{spinellis2003decay}, and Lawrence and colleagues had found the same
fragility throughout the scientific literature~\autocite{lawrence2001persistence}. Digital
information decays if left untended --- the ``bit rot'' and ``digital dark age'' that
preservationists have warned of for a generation~\autocite{cerf2011avoiding,anderson2015digital}
--- and source code, with its web of dependencies and its decades-long lives, decays faster than
most. Keeping it is a job in its own right~\autocite{matthews2010framework}, and it is not the
job a forge ever signed up for.

\section{The universal archive}

If forges will not keep our code, something must. Software Heritage is that something: an
international, non-profit, long-term initiative whose mission is to \emph{collect, preserve, and
share} the source code of all publicly available software --- a modern Library of Alexandria for
code, built once and shared by industry, research, culture, and society as a
whole~\autocite{SwhCACM2018,SWHecosystems2023}.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=.86\linewidth]{figures/archive-growth-2026-03-10.png}
  \caption[Growth of the Software Heritage archive]{The growth of the Software Heritage archive
  over time: by early 2026 it holds the development history of hundreds of millions of projects
  --- the largest corpus of source code ever assembled.}
  \label{fig:archive-growth}
\end{figure}

It fills itself automatically, by a division of labour worth understanding
(Figure~\ref{fig:dataflow}). \emph{Listers} walk a hosting platform and enumerate the repositories
(the \emph{origins}) it holds; \emph{loaders} then ingest each origin --- its files, its
directories, and, crucially, its full commit history --- whatever the version-control system (Git,
Mercurial, Subversion) or package format (a Debian source package, a PyPI tarball, \ldots),
\emph{canonicalizing} them all into the one uniform data model described below. The harvesting runs
across more than 5{,}000 distinct code-hosting and distribution platforms --- the big public forges,
per-institution GitLab and Gitea servers, Linux-distribution archives, and language package
registries. New listers and
loaders are contributed by a broad community, with support from the Alfred P.\ Sloan and NLnet
foundations. The result, at the time of writing, is hard to overstate: the development history of
more than \emph{28 billion} unique source files from over \emph{430 million} projects, some three
petabytes of deduplicated content, organised as roughly sixty billion nodes joined by close to a
trillion edges (Figure~\ref{fig:archive-growth}). It is, by a wide margin, the largest body of
source code ever gathered in one place.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/swh-dataflow.pdf}
  \caption[How Software Heritage harvests and canonicalizes code]{How the archive fills itself, and
  \emph{canonicalizes} as it goes. \emph{Listers} enumerate the repositories (\emph{origins}) held by
  a hosting platform --- forges (GitHub, GitLab, Bitbucket, Gitea), Linux distributions (Debian,
  Ubuntu, Fedora), and language package registries (PyPI, npm, CPAN, \ldots), across more than
  5{,}000 such platforms. \emph{Loaders} then ingest each origin, whatever its version-control system (Git,
  Mercurial, Subversion) or package format (Debian \texttt{.dsc}, PyPI tarballs, \ldots), and convert
  them all into \emph{one} uniform representation: the Merkle DAG at right, with identical files and
  directories deduplicated across every origin. Heterogeneous sources in; a single, canonical model
  out.}
  \label{fig:dataflow}
\end{figure}

The interesting question is not how big the archive is, but how it is \emph{organised} --- because
the organization is what makes it more than a very large backup.

\section{The Merkle DAG data model}

Archiving the world's code so that anyone can \emph{identify} a precise piece of it calls for a
particular kind of identifier. We want identifiers that are \emph{intrinsic} --- derived from the
content itself, not handed out by a registry --- and that work not only for single files but for
the \emph{tree structures}, directories-within-directories, that are the natural shape of
software. A classical construction gives us exactly this. We meet it first in the abstract, then
name the five kinds of object Software Heritage builds from it, and finally watch it at work on a
real repository.

\subsection{What a Merkle DAG is}

Ralph Merkle, looking for an efficient way to authenticate many items at once, proposed
marrying a tree to a cryptographic hash function: label every leaf with the hash of its data, then
label every internal node with the hash of \emph{its children's labels}, repeating all the way to
the root~\autocite{merkle1987digital}. The resulting \emph{Merkle tree}
(Figure~\ref{fig:merkle-tree}) has a remarkable property: the label of the root is a fingerprint of
the \emph{entire} structure beneath it. Change a single byte in a single leaf and every label on the
path from that leaf to the root changes with it --- the root's label simply cannot stay the same
unless every byte below it is unchanged.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=.8\linewidth]{figures/merkle-tree.pdf}
  \caption[A Merkle tree]{A Merkle tree, in Merkle's original construction. Each data block
  ($L_1$--$L_4$) is hashed; each internal node holds the hash of its children's hashes; and the
  single \emph{Top Hash} fingerprints the entire structure. Change any block and every hash on the
  path up to the root changes with it. Software Heritage applies the same idea to files and
  directories --- and, by storing identical subtrees once, turns the tree into a DAG.}
  \label{fig:merkle-tree}
\end{figure}

Software adds one twist a plain tree does not capture: the same file, and the same directory,
recur \emph{everywhere} --- the identical license text sitting in a thousand projects, the
identical sub-library \emph{vendored} --- copied wholesale --- into hundreds. Because a node's identifier is computed purely from its
content, two identical subtrees receive the \emph{same} identifier, so they can be stored once and
pointed at from every place they occur. A tree with this sharing is no longer a tree but a
\emph{directed acyclic graph}: a Merkle DAG. (It stays acyclic for a simple reason --- a node's
identifier depends on its children's identifiers, so no node can ever contain itself.)

This single idea buys three properties we could not otherwise have together. Such identifiers are
\emph{gratis} --- you can mint billions of them at no cost; they guarantee \emph{integrity} ---
change one byte and the identifier changes, so an object cannot be altered without its identifier
changing; and they need \emph{no middle-man} --- no central authority has to exist for the identifier
to mean something. Integrity \emph{and} no middle-man, at once, is exactly the combination a registry
cannot give you --- which is why the same construction underpins Git, Nix, IPFS, and the
blockchains.

\takeaway{Gratis, integrity, no middle-man --- a Merkle DAG delivers all three at once.}

\subsection{The five kinds of node}

Software Heritage stores \emph{all} the code it has ever collected, with its full history, in
\emph{one} such Merkle DAG~\autocite{ICMS2020,pietri2019graph}. Its nodes come in five kinds.
Each is identified by the hash of its own content, and each is, in the main, built out of the kind
below it --- so the five form a layered structure, contents at the bottom and whole-repository
snapshots at the top (Figure~\ref{fig:merkle-layers}):

\begin{description}[leftmargin=!,labelwidth=2.4cm,font=\bfseries\sffamily]
  \item[content (\texttt{cnt})] the raw bytes of a single file --- a \emph{blob}, in Git's
        vocabulary. It is a leaf of the DAG: its identifier is computed directly from its bytes, and it
        knows nothing of its own filename.
  \item[directory (\texttt{dir})] a list of named entries, each pointing at a content (a file) or
        another directory (a subdirectory), together with its permissions. Its identifier is the hash of
        that list, so it fixes the \emph{exact} contents and layout of the tree beneath it.
  \item[revision (\texttt{rev})] a commit: a directory (the snapshot of the source tree at that
        point) plus metadata --- author, date, message, and a link to the parent revision(s). The
        chain of parents is the project's history.
  \item[release (\texttt{rel})] a named, often signed, milestone --- a tagged revision such as
        \texttt{v2.3.0} --- carrying its own author, date, and annotation.
  \item[snapshot (\texttt{snp})] the complete state of a repository \emph{as archived at one
        visit}: the full list of its branches and tags and what each pointed to at that moment. It
        is the entry point from which everything else hangs.
\end{description}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=.82\linewidth]{figures/swh-merkle-overview.png}
  \caption[The five node types as one layered DAG]{The five kinds of node, layered into one Merkle
  DAG. From the top: \emph{origins} (the repositories crawled) point at \emph{snapshots}, which point
  at \emph{releases} and \emph{revisions}, which point at \emph{directories}, which point at the
  \emph{contents} (file blobs) at the bottom. Because every node is identified by the hash of
  everything beneath it, a node reached from several parents --- the same directory or file shared
  across revisions and across repositories --- is stored exactly once.}
  \label{fig:merkle-layers}
\end{figure}

\subsection{A worked example: hashing your way up}

The five layers are easiest to believe when you watch them being built, bottom to top, on a real
repository. The figures that follow do exactly that, and they share one running thread: the
\textsc{gpl}\,v3 license file, whose Git-style hash \texttt{94a9ed02\ldots} you can follow as it
is carried upward from layer to layer.

Start with a \textbf{content} (Figure~\ref{fig:merkle-cnt}). Feed a file --- here the text of the
\textsc{gpl}\,v3 --- to a hash function and you get several digests; the one Software Heritage uses
as the node's identifier is the Git-style \texttt{sha1\_git} (in red, \texttt{94a9ed02\ldots}). That
hash \emph{is} the identifier of this file's content, anywhere it ever appears.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=.72\linewidth]{figures/merkle-1-contents.pdf}
  \caption[A content node]{A \texttt{content} node: the raw bytes of a file (the \textsc{gpl}\,v3
  license) hashed to an identifier. Software Heritage uses the Git-style \texttt{sha1\_git}
  (\texttt{94a9ed02\ldots}, red) --- so this content's \textsc{swhid} will be
  \swhid{swh:1:cnt:94a9ed02\ldots}, exactly the example met in the next chapter.}
  \label{fig:merkle-cnt}
\end{figure}

Now gather files into a \textbf{directory} (Figure~\ref{fig:merkle-dir}). A directory is just a
list: one line per entry, giving its permissions, its type (\texttt{blob} for a file,
\texttt{tree} for a subdirectory), the \emph{identifier of the object it points to}, and the entry's
own filename. Notice the \texttt{LICENSE} line: it carries precisely the \texttt{94a9ed02\ldots}
we just computed. Hash this whole listing and you get the directory's identifier,
\texttt{515f00d4\ldots} --- an identifier that pins down every file in the tree and exactly which bytes
each contains.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=.9\linewidth]{figures/merkle-2-directories.pdf}
  \caption[A directory node]{A \texttt{directory} node: a list of entries, each naming a
  \texttt{content} (file) or a \texttt{tree} (subdirectory). The \texttt{LICENSE} entry points at
  the very content of Figure~\ref{fig:merkle-cnt} (\texttt{94a9ed02\ldots}); hashing the whole
  list yields the directory identifier \texttt{515f00d4\ldots}.}
  \label{fig:merkle-dir}
\end{figure}

A \textbf{revision} (Figure~\ref{fig:merkle-rev}) wraps a directory in history. Its content is a
small record: a \texttt{tree} line --- here \texttt{515f00d4\ldots}, the very directory we just
built --- a link to the parent revision, the author and committer, and the commit message.
Hashing that record gives the commit's identifier, \texttt{963634dc\ldots}: change the tree, the
message, or the parent, and the commit is a different commit, with a different identifier.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=.86\linewidth]{figures/merkle-3-revisions.pdf}
  \caption[A revision node]{A \texttt{revision} (commit) node: a \texttt{tree} (the directory of
  Figure~\ref{fig:merkle-dir}, \texttt{515f00d4\ldots}), a parent link, author/committer, and a
  message --- hashed to the revision identifier \texttt{963634dc\ldots}.}
  \label{fig:merkle-rev}
\end{figure}

Two further layers sit on top. A \textbf{release} (Figure~\ref{fig:merkle-rel}) is a named,
usually annotated and signed, marker attached to one revision --- the object behind a tag such as
\texttt{v0.0.51}. And a \textbf{snapshot} (Figure~\ref{fig:merkle-snp}) records the \emph{whole}
state of a repository at the moment it was archived: every branch and tag, and the object each
pointed to --- exactly what \texttt{git show-ref} would print. The snapshot is the root from which
a complete archived repository can be recovered.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=.82\linewidth]{figures/merkle-4-releases.pdf}
  \caption[A release node]{A \texttt{release} node: an annotated, signed tag (\texttt{v0.0.51})
  pointing at one revision, with its own tagger, date, and message --- hashed to the release identifier
  \texttt{85083a5c\ldots}.}
  \label{fig:merkle-rel}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=.85\linewidth]{figures/merkle-5-snapshots.pdf}
  \caption[A snapshot node]{A \texttt{snapshot} node: the full set of a repository's branches and
  tags and what each pointed to at archival time (the output of \texttt{git show-ref}) --- hashed
  to the snapshot identifier \texttt{b464cad1\ldots}.}
  \label{fig:merkle-snp}
\end{figure}

Stack these five layers and let identical subtrees be shared, and you have the whole picture:
\emph{one} Merkle DAG in which contents feed directories, directories feed revisions, and those
feed releases and snapshots (Figure~\ref{fig:merkle-dag}). Because every node is identified by the hash
of everything below it, identical files and directories --- of which the world's software has a
staggering number --- are stored exactly once, and the archive as a whole becomes, in effect, a
global ledger of public code whose integrity anyone can verify~\autocite{SWHecosystems2023}.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=.84\linewidth]{figures/swh-merkle-dag.pdf}
  \caption[The Software Heritage Merkle DAG]{The Software Heritage data model: one Merkle directed
  acyclic graph in which file \emph{contents} (\texttt{cnt}) are gathered into \emph{directories}
  (\texttt{dir}), directories into \emph{revisions} (\texttt{rev}, i.e.\ commits), and those into
  \emph{releases} (\texttt{rel}) and \emph{snapshots} (\texttt{snp}) --- the five layers of the
  worked example above. Each node's identifier is the hash of everything beneath it; identical subtrees
  are stored once.}
  \label{fig:merkle-dag}
\end{figure}

That is the foundation. We now have somewhere to keep code, and a data model in which every
artifact already \emph{has} an identifier, computed from its content. The next chapter takes that
identifier seriously --- and shows how it untangles a confusion that has dogged digital identifiers
for decades.
